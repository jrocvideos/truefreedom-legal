Every April, millions of people across North America sit down and hand over a significant portion of what they earned.

Not because they verified the law. Not because they read the Constitution. But because everyone else does it. Because they're afraid of what happens if they don't. Because they've been told it's their duty.

But what if the fear was the point?

---

According to Peymon Mottahedeh of Freedom Law School — a legal researcher who has spent over 30 years studying US tax law — there is no constitutional amendment or federal law on the books that requires the average American to file and pay federal income taxes.

That's not a fringe conspiracy theory. That's a constitutional argument backed by legal research.

The claim centers on this: when the modern income tax system was written in 1913, it was deliberately structured to apply to residents of Washington DC and federal territories — not to citizens of the 50 states.

> "The founding fathers were freedom lovers. They did not have a revolution and create a federal government to make us slaves of this new entity they created."

The 16th Amendment — the one the government claims gives it taxing power over all Americans — was ruled by the US Supreme Court to have given Congress no new taxing power.

So what happened?

They made you believe it was your duty. They made compliance feel patriotic. And for over a hundred years — it worked.

---

**This isn't just an American problem.**

In Canada, the UK, Australia — the same pattern exists. Tax systems built on voluntary compliance dressed up as legal obligation. Enforcement through fear, not law.

When you ask most people why they pay income tax, the answer is some version of: *"Because I have to."*

But when pressed for the specific law — the exact statute that compels them — most can't name it.

Because the system wasn't designed to be questioned.

It was designed to be obeyed.

---

**The real question isn't whether to pay taxes.**

It's whether you've ever actually verified that you're legally required to — or whether you've simply assumed it because everyone around you has done the same.

Sovereignty begins with asking questions the system hopes you never think to ask.

> Freedom doesn't disappear overnight. It fades when people stop asking who gave the government the authority it claims to have.
